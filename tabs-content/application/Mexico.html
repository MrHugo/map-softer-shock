AGRI-FOOD SECTOR

INDUSTRIAL PROJECTS

Mexico has been planting biotech crops since 1996, and is one of the six pioneer with USA, Brazil, Canada, India and Argentina and biotech planting countries.1

Since 1996, Mexico had 158 approvals for GMOs food cultivars and feed use.

In 2016 Mexico planted 101,000 hectares of biotech crops, down from 141,000 hectares in 2015. The 101,000 hectares are cotton and soybean, cotton being one of the most important GMO crop grown in Mexico.1

However, there are still negative propaganda and cultural prejudices that opponents use to confuse the public about the technology, it happens the same in other countries such as France.

In conclusion, to increase biotech projects in Mexico we need involved stakeholders and effective communication with the general public in order to address marketing issues and support scientific research and industrial investment in applications of biotechnology.

Currently, a few companies in Mexico are demonstrating some of the practical possibilities that synthetic biology can offers:1

  • The National Institute of Forestry, Agriculture and Livestock Research (INIFAP) has developed GMO beans (Phaseolus vulgaris) with resistance to fungi Colletorichum lindemuthianum, Fusarium lateritium and Rhizoctonia solani. The beans were tested in Celaya, Guanajuato after a permit for experimental release was granted in 2014.
  • The International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center (CIMMYT) have tested experimental releases of GM wheat over the last seven years with various traits including drought tolerance.
  • The National Laboratory of Genomics for Biodiversity (LANGEBIO), Advanced Studies (CINVESTAV) and a private company are developing biotech crops that will absorb and optimize the use of phosphorus.
  • CINVESTAV is also developing a GMO lemon tree (Citrus aurantifolia) resistant to the disease known as Huanglongbing (HLB) which causes losses of lemon crops each year.

Biotechnology in the environmental sector is very beneficial for Mexico;

Since 1996, biotech cotton farmers from Chihuahua have saved 30 percent on their production costs due to reduced pesticide use, going from 18 applications per growing season to just one.2

Biotech improvements to soybean and cotton are estimated to have increased Mexican revenues by US$489 million from 1996 to 2015. The profits alone for 2015 are estimated at US$77 million (Brookes and Barfoot).

IGEM PROJECTS

iGEM 2010 - Tec-Monterrey

The iGEM 2010 Tec-Monterrey team from Mexico developed a new concentrated bacterial sensor using genetically modified microorganisms. Using genetic engineering has resulted in more sophisticated bacterial reporters that will be capable of detecting more easily the presence of contaminants, sugars and amino acids in different media such as soil and water.

They won the gold medal and if you want to visit their wiki.

iGEM 2012 - UANL_Mty

The 2012 iGEM UANL_Mty team from Mexico, created a system using an arsenic biosensor and a chelator made by preior iGEM teams for bio-remediation of heavy metal and metalloid contamination. They did several lectures and activities with school students to share knowledge and teach them synthetic biology. They performed several DNA extractions among other introductory experiments.

They won the gold medal and if you want to visit their wiki.

HEALTH SECTOR

INDUSTRIAL PROJECTS

In October 2009, Mexican researchers and companies directors discussed about the future development of applied synthetic biology, reaching two main conclusions. First, Mexico is is relatively late in examining these issues. Secondly, the research directions that would allow Mexico to be competitive were not clearly defined. http://www.biosintetica.mx

Since then Mexico has seen the formation of associations such as Biosintetica to promote synthetic biology research and technological development in the Mexican academic and industrial sectors.

Despite this, Mexico has made important contributions to synthetic biology.

In 1980, the Mexican scientist Francisco Bolivar Zapata was able to access the DNA of a pancreatic cell and from there obtained the gene that encodes insulin.

Thanks to the isolation of this gene, it could be inserted into a vector called pBR 322. The vector and the insert were incorporated into a recombinant bacterium. Subsequently they were put into a fermentation tank and with this it was possible the unlimited reproduction of the high purity insulin.

Current Mexican synthetic biology projects:

In general, no established companies use genetic engineering in the health sector.3

However, several Mexican universities have departments that work with synthetic biology such as:

  • The center of research CINVESTAV in Irapuato has a department of genetic engineering active in several health sector applications of synthetic biology.4
  • “Red synbioMX” is The National Synthetic Biology Network of Mexico. It is presented as a web page made from a collective effort of researchers and students working in synthetic biology in Mexico and abroad. It is an non-profit interface with the goal of keeping the Mexican society informed about synthetic biology news.5

For example, a group of graduate students from the National Polytechnic Institute in Guanajuato created GATCorp which is the first start-up of synthetic biology in Mexico. The purpose of GATCorp is to incorporate biotechnological processes in the natural and chemical products’ industry in Mexico.6

Red synbioMX has the goal of popularizing synthetic biology through activities, lectures and conferences to unite all sectors that work on synthetic biology. For more information and the latest news about synthetic biology projects in Mexico, visit their web page.

  • “Bread for all”developed in Spain and Mexico,is a project in the health sector that is being developed to find wheat varieties for people allergic to gluten.

Celiac disease, or "gluten intolerance", affects 1% of consumers. The recent CRISPR / Cas9 technique offers the hope of being able to modify the epitopes involved in celiac disease and to lead to acceptable varieties, that is to say to low levels of gliadins guilty of allergies, and thus to be consumed by the sick. Trials are under way for 10 and 20 people with celiac disease respectively.7

They have a SynthechBio Network made by reserachers that supports biohacking and do it yourself-biology initiatives (DIY-Bio). SynthechBio has been communicating and organizing activities in several South American countries such as Peru, Argentina and Brazil to promote synthetic biology.8

For more information, visit their web page.

IGEM PROJECTS

iGEM 2014 - ITESM-CEM Mexico

The iGEM 2014 ITESM-CEM team from Mexico worked on a preventive gene therapy for atherosclerosis using microbial enzymes. This project was motivated by the fact that the human body is unable to degrade many substances that affect our health and so these substances start to accumulate slowly and finally reduce our lifespan. They try to build a system using synthetic biology to degrade oxidized cholesterol. They also did several talks and courses about their project, about molecular and synthetic biology, and the positive impacts they could have on society.

Visit their wiki.

iGEM 2013 - BIOSINT_MEXICO

The iGEM 2013 BIOSINT team from Mexico developed a smart probiotic that included a sensor for xenobiotic substances that will detect and degrade pesticides in the body. This project has the goal to provide possible solutions for health issues in Mexico.

Visit their wiki.
