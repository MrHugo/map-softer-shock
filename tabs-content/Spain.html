Spain

Spain is one of the biggest producer of GMO crops in the EU with 0.1 million of hectares of biotech crops in 2016.1 As a member of the European Union, Spain is strongly affected by the EU rules, the first one being the directive 2001/18/EC, which regulates the release of GMO on the environment and the regulation (EC) No 1829/2003, which defines the modified feed and food that can be placed on the market and can be consumed.2 Moreover, a national law (Law 9/2003 and the royal decree 178/2004) was also voted to provide other restrictions on GMOs. These laws led to the creation of the National Biosafety Commission and the Inter-Ministerial Council for Genetically Modified Organisms.3

Also, to prevent the risk of a potential release of GMO in the environment, international convention has been made that leads to the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety on 2000. This protocol can help countries to have a basis regulation and methodology for modified organisms, to harmonize the regulation on biosafety on an international level, and a mechanism for international information exchange on biosafety.4,5 Spain ratified the Cartagena protocol on 2002.6

Research:

To practice research on biotechnology, an authorization of the National Biosafety Commission is needed. If the experiments take place at a national level, an authorization of the Inter-Ministerial Council for Genetically Modified Organisms is also required.7

Cultivation:

To cultivate GMOs an authorization of the National Biosafety Commission and the Inter-Ministerial Council for Genetically Modified Organisms is necessary, specific equipment is needed to cultivate, transport, store the crops, and a minimum distance of 220 meters is necessary between GMO and non-GMO crops. Before the beginning of the production, the neighborhood and the local authorities must be notified of the future GMO crops.

Also, rapports must be send to the ministry of environment by the company, the National Biosafety Commission and the Inter-Ministerial Council for Genetically Modified Organisms to keep track of the location of the GMO and their effects on the environment.8,9

Commercialization:

To commercialize GMOs, an authorization of the National Biosafety Commission and the Inter-Ministerial Council for Genetically Modified Organisms. Only the GMOs approved by the EU can be imported.
Once the GMO receive the authorization to be commercialized, the product is labelled in function of the European law. If the final product contains more than 0.9% of GMO food, the product must be labelled as GMO product.7,10

Consumption:

The consumption of GMO food in Spain follows the European law. Only products that are granted by the European Union as safe for the human health and environment can be bought.7

AGRI-FOOD SECTOR

INDUSTRIAL PROJECTS

1998: Spain started using GM maize.

Since then, Spain has qualified as one of the 18 biotech “mega-countries” growing more than 50,000 hectares.1

Spain remains one of the few EU countries that allows the use of GM crops and has become the highest yielding producer in EU.

The autonomous regions of Aragon and Catalonia had the largest share of biotech maize (70%) of Spain’s total biotech maize plantings, as the corn borer insect is endemic in these areas.

Today, some companies in Spain are demonstrating the possibilities that synthetic biology can offer through fundamental and applied research projects:

  • In Madrid,the CSIC (Consejo Superior de Investigaciones Científicas) built a research center called “CNB” (Centro Nacional de Biotechnologia) in 1992. Many prestigious Spanish researcher work in this center on several projects in synthetic biology such as Victor de Lorenzo who is one of the main drivers of synthetic biology in Spain.2

They are developing multiple biological agents for biosensing, remediation, and transformation of urban and industrial chemical waste that is dumped into the environment.

One of the tools they use is the bacterium Pseudomonas putida, easy to work with and a reliable chassis for implantation of genetic and metabolic circuits.

Perspectives for the future 3:

  • BioCassava Plus Project: Improve the nutritional qualities of cassava.
  • Africa Biofortified Sorghum Project: Develop a more nutritious and easily digestible sorghum that contains increased levels of amino acids, vitamins, iron and zinc. Sorghum is the fifth most important cereal crop and the main dietary staple for more than 500 million people.
  • Water Efficient Maize for Africa (WEMA) Project: Developing drought-tolerant maize, a staple that more that more 300 million Africans depend on as their main food source.

How does synthetic biology impact Spain?4

  • Economic benefits or farmers due to higher yields and a lower cost of inputs.
  • Water savings equivalent to the use of almost 750.000 citizens.
  • Farmers margins improved by up to 147 € per hectare.

IGEM PROJECTS

iGEM 2013 - Valencia-CIPF

In 2013, the iGEM team Valencia-CIPF worked on developing a device to create an organism capable of repelling insects.

This device will be used in areas of Africa, Asia, and the Americas that suffer from diseases like malaria, sleeping sickness or cholera.

They used the properties of monoterpenes from Arabodpsis thaliana (able to repel some insects) and the S-linalool synthase gene from Clarkia breweri, responsible for the aroma. They introduce the gene responsible inside a yeast so that the repellent would always be expressed.

Their work is described in more detail on their web page.

iGEM 2016 - UPO_SEVILLLA

The iGEM 2016 team UPO SEVILLA, worked on a project to eliminate glycerol from the environment by using it as a carbon source for bacteria. The microorganism they used was Pseudomonas putida, capable of adhering to a substrate and aggregating to form communities or biofilms. Their goal was to produce compounds that are beneficial for human beings. They genetically manipulate P. putida to increase glycerol assimilation and to control formation and dispersal of biofilms.

Their work is described in more detail on their web page.

HEALTH SECTOR

INDUSTRIAL PROJECTS

Nowadays, some companies in Spain are doing projects to show the possibilities that synthetic biology can offer in the health sector through projects:

  • Victor Lorenzo is working in the development of an artificial immune system using synthetic biology at the CNB.1
    Scientists say that artificial bacteria will be able to reprogram stem cells and regenerate all types of tissues or damaged organs, so they can instantly detect and treat tumour cells.2
  • Francis Mojica researcher at the University of Alicante, who discovered that bacteria use a method to develop immunity that opened the door to revolution in genetic manipulation. He discovered the CRISPR-Cas9 system. The technique, today worth billions of dollars, came from the study of microbes in Santa Pola.

This year, genetic testing will be tested against diseases such as cancer or HIV in Spain.

  • The Research Group on Synthesis of active principles and chemistry of steroids at the University of Valladolid (UVa), led by Professor Alfonso Pérez Encabo works on developing new products and new synthetic routes using steroids for the the pharmaceutical industry.3
  • Ricard Solé principal investigator of the University Pompeu Fabra (UPF), proposes the use of synthetic biology to counteract the anthropogenic effects on our planet through the design of bacteria that degrade plastic polymers. 4
  • Wheat varieties for people allergic to gluten "Bread for all" is an other project developed in Spain and Mexico.

Celiac disease, or "gluten intolerance", affects 1% of consumers. The recent CRISPR / Cas9 technique offers the hope of being able to modify the epitopes involved in celiac disease and to lead to acceptable varieties, that is to say to low levels of gliadins guilty of allergies, and thus to be consumed by the sick. Trials are under way for 10 and 20 people with celiac disease respectively.5

They have two main approaches to designing microorganisms:6

  1. Modification of existing organisms and the creation of a dependence between them necessary to survive (obligate mutualism). This strategy could make it possible to avoid the transition to a desert state in arid ecosystems (40% of the planet's ecosystems).
  2. Design of organisms capable of surviving in a specific substrate, such as industrial waste. Proper design would allow these organisms to be viable only in these artificial media.

The same group is working on biological computers, groups of genetically modified cells that act in part following conventional binary logic but with a design that moves away from both electronics and biological designs. This could permit applications related to "intelligent" biosensors or the creation of diagnostic kits, for example.

IGEM PROJECTS

iGEM 2016 - UPF-CRG Barcelona

The team UPF-CRG from Barcelona, performed in 2016 a project in developing a probiotic that could reduce the amount of polyamines that our body absorb. These polyamines in high concentrations are responsible of in a long term increase the probabilities of developing cancer, they are found in processed meats among other dishes.

Their probiotic will consist of a mix of different modified bacteria that will simply process the excess part of polyamines from the digestion and eliminate them using a set of natural enzymes also found in our bodies. The probiotic will be consumed as an oral pill. They had also planned to develop a cancer risk detector by engineering a bacterial cell. In that case they will use the acetylated version of polyamines that have been targeted in previous studies to have a high success in both sensibility and specificity of cancer risk detection.

Their work is described in more detail on their web page.

iGEM 2011 - UPO_SEVILLA

The team UPO from Sevilla, developed in 2011, molecular systems that reproduce an electronic flip-flop (a device that switches between two different states (0 to 1) allowing the construction of living memory devices closer to the robustness of informatics. The idea came from prior iGEM teams, so they took their system and improved it by constructing different regulation levels in a different model organisms such as bacteria or yeast. They won a prize for the Best Human Practice project in Europe in which they created a Spanish web page to communicate about synthetic biology in Spain.

Their work is described in more detail on their web page.
