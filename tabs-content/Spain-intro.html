Spain is one of the biggest producer of GMO crops in the EU with 0.1 million of hectares of biotech crops in 2016.1 As a member of the European Union, Spain is strongly affected by the EU rules, the first one being the directive 2001/18/EC, which regulates the release of GMO on the environment and the regulation (EC) No 1829/2003, which defines the modified feed and food that can be placed on the market and can be consumed.2 Moreover, a national law (Law 9/2003 and the royal decree 178/2004) was also voted to provide other restrictions on GMOs. These laws led to the creation of the National Biosafety Commission and the Inter-Ministerial Council for Genetically Modified Organisms.3

Also, to prevent the risk of a potential release of GMO in the environment, international convention has been made that leads to the Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety on 2000. This protocol can help countries to have a basis regulation and methodology for modified organisms, to harmonize the regulation on biosafety on an international level, and a mechanism for international information exchange on biosafety.4,5 Spain ratified the Cartagena protocol on 2002.6
